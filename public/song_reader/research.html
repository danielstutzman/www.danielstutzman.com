Research in Optical Music Recognition

Optical Music Recognition (OMR) converts a picture of sheet music into a format that can be edited and played. It’s related to Optical Character Recognition (OCR), which converts a picture of text into a format that can be edited or searched.

OMR and OCR are both open research areas in computer vision, as they haven’t yet been definitively solved. However, in many situations the current techniques are accurate enough to be usable.

OMR software products have been commercially available since the 1990’s, but they make you feed the music score through a scanner connected to a traditional computer. Song Reader requires only a camera phone. This makes the research task more challenging for a few reasons:

Horizontal lines are straight, because the scanner’s physical design flattens the paper and keeps it from turning.

 Horizontal lines are slanted, because it’s impractical to hold the camera perfectly aligned, and bound volumes might not lie flat.
The paper is much brighter than the ink on it, because scanners have a light source inside. The paper is similar in brightness to the ink on it, because light sources are farther away and maybe blocked.
Symbols are easily recognizable, because the scanner’s camera is close to the paper, and its motor controls the paper’s movement. Symbols are blurry, because a person must attempt to hold the camera still a short distance from the paper. Also JPEG image compression degrades the image.
